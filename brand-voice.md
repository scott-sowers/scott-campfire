# Brand Voice — Scott Sowers

## Customer-Facing Communication

### Tone
- Professional but approachable — never stiff or overly corporate
- Confident without being pushy — I'm a trusted advisor, not a used-car salesman
- Warm and genuine — I actually care about solving their problems
- Concise — respect their time, get to the point

### Email Style
**Assume every email is read on a phone, standing up, between meetings.** Write accordingly.

- Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max). Discovery recaps under 200 words total.
- Open with first name + one warm line: "Great connecting today." / "Thanks for making time Friday."
- **Discovery recaps follow a fixed structure:** What I Heard → What We'll Cover in the Demo → Next Steps
- Bullets are punchy fragments using the prospect's own words — not full sentences, not marketing copy
- One confident bridge sentence between pain and solution: "We can absolutely help here." + one line of specifics
- Close with: "Anything I missed or mischaracterized? Let me know either way."
- Sign off: "Best, Scott" or "Talk soon, Scott"
- Never use "Just checking in," "Circling back," "Per our discussion," "I hope this finds you well," or "Please don't hesitate to reach out"
- Pricing responses: give the number first, then context, then CTA. No buildup.
- Champion enablement follow-ups: link + 2 sentences. Let the asset do the work.
- Stalled deals: name the stall directly, offer an easy out. "No wrong answer, I just want to make sure I'm not filling your inbox if the timing isn't right."

### Discovery Call Approach
- Start with genuine curiosity about their business
- Ask open-ended questions: "Tell me about...", "Walk me through..."
- Key discovery topics: their role, why Campfire, what else they're evaluating, tech stack, multi-entity/currency needs, timeline, AI initiatives, IPO aspirations
- Use the 3-pillar discovery framework: Finance Operating Model → Cost of Pain → Why Now (full framework in reference/CLAUDE.md)
- Always transition to scheduling the demo before ending the call

### Language Patterns That Win (from Messaging Analysis)
- Say "AI-native" not "AI-powered" — signals built-in, not bolted-on
- Say "finance operating system" not "ERP" — broader, more modern positioning
- Use speed-to-value messaging: "weeks, not months" / "live in 30 days"
- Pain amplification: tie manual processes to dollar cost ("15 days to close = $X in delayed decisions")
- Name the enemy: "spreadsheet tax," "customization entropy," "consultant dependency"
- Use social proof: "Companies like [similar company] made the switch in [timeframe]"
- Avoid generic superlatives — use specific metrics and concrete examples instead

### CTA Patterns by Stage
- **Discovery → Demo:** "Based on what you've shared about [specific pain], I think a focused demo on [pillar] would be the best next step. Does [day/time] work?"
- **Demo → Pricing:** "I'd love to put together a proposal tailored to [their specific needs]. Can we set up 30 minutes next week?"
- **Pricing → Close:** "We can lock in [specific offer/discount] if we can get this wrapped by [date]. What does your internal approval process look like?"
- **Stalled deal:** Reference call offer ("Would it help to talk to [similar company] who went through the same evaluation?")

### Proposal & Deck Language
- Lead with the business problem, not features
- Use their language and terminology back to them
- Include specific ROI examples when possible
- Keep slides minimal — heavy content goes in appendix
- Reference the Three Pillars structure: Revenue Automation → Core Accounting → Ember AI

## Internal Communication

### Slack Style
- Casual, direct, brief
- Use threads to keep channels clean
- Tag people when they need to act
- Emoji reactions to acknowledge without cluttering

### Reports & Updates
- Lead with the headline (what changed, what matters)
- Bullet points for details
- Always include "What I need" if there's an ask
