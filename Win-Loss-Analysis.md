# Campfire Win/Loss Analysis — HubSpot Deal Review
**Date:** March 3, 2026 | **Analyst:** Claude (for Scott Sowers) | **Segment Focus:** Enterprise (501+ FTE)

---

## Executive Summary

After reviewing 10 recent closed-won deals and 15+ closed-lost deals in HubSpot, clear patterns emerge around **why Campfire wins** and **where deals break down**. The biggest takeaway: Campfire wins when there's an identified finance champion driving the evaluation, and loses most often to **Rillet on price** or to **bad-fit qualification failures** early in the funnel.

### Winning Pattern #1: Strong Finance Champion = Faster Close

8 of 10 closed-won deals had an **identified champion** — and they were overwhelmingly **finance operators** (Controllers, VP Finance, Head of Finance). The two deals without named champions (Truss Payments and Forge Vista) were either renewals or had lighter qualification notes.

**Key champion titles that close:** Controller, VP of Finance & Operations, Head of Finance, Finance Leader. These are the people who *feel the pain daily* of manual close processes, broken rev rec, and Excel sprawl.

### Winning Pattern #2: Multi-Entity & Integration Complexity Drives Value

The highest-value deals all involved **multi-entity consolidation** and **multiple integrations**:

- **100 Thieves** ($10K but 3 entities, historical migration back to 2017, integrations to payroll/CRM/inventory) — long sales cycle but comprehensive scope
- **Expand-ia** ($20K, replacing NetSuite across 5 entities, Stripe/Paychex/Ramp integrations)
- **Forge Vista Capital** ($19K, replacing QuickBooks across 6 entities, intercompany eliminations)
- **Macabacus** ($40K, replacing Maxio for B2B order-to-cash + rev rec across multi-system integrations)
- **Dealops** ($60K, referral — highest ACV, minimal competition)

**Takeaway:** When Campfire solves multi-entity complexity, deal sizes go up and the competitive moat is widest.

Notably, the two deals where **Rillet was the competition** and Campfire still won (Rythm Health, Healthie) were both **outbound, SDR-sourced**. This suggests the SDR team is doing effective work identifying and engaging prospects before Rillet can anchor on price.

### Winning Pattern #4: "Replace Legacy" Narrative Resonates

Deals where the buyer was **actively replacing** an existing system closed with conviction:

- Expand-ia: replacing **NetSuite** (5 entities)
- Forge Vista Capital: replacing **QuickBooks** (6 entities)
- Macabacus: replacing **Maxio** (order-to-cash)
- Healthie: automating on top of **Stripe + Ramp** (manual processes)
- CapIntel: replacing multiple disconnected finance tools

The "$5M NetSuite Buyout Fund" value prop likely resonated for Expand-ia. For QuickBooks replacements, the "outgrown SMB tool" messaging works.

### Winning Pattern #5: Speed Kills (in a Good Way)

The fastest closes were Dealops (16 days), Mutiny (15 days), and Macabacus (15 days) — all inbound or referral with clear, pre-existing pain. When prospects arrive with urgency, Campfire's sales motion can close in 2-3 weeks.

## Part 2: Closed-Lost Analysis (15 Deals)

### Loss Category Breakdown

| Loss Reason | Count | Key Examples |
|-------------|-------|-------------|
| **Bad Fit / Non-ICP** | 5 | Willis Law Group (law firm), Bentiu (construction/oil), Ziina (Dubai, no integrations), Kosli (intl accounting), G5 Entertainment |
| **Budget / Price** | 6 | Succinct, Novig, Hive, Goodside, Civil Designer, Edge Business Services |
| **Went Cold** | 3 | Datatruck, Signal Advisors, Lore Health |
| **Churn (Renewal Loss)** | 1 | Cercli |
| **Lost to Competitor** | 1 (tagged) | Ideogram (lost to Rillet) |

### Failure Pattern #1: Rillet Is Winning on Price — Aggressively

The single most important competitive insight: **Rillet is undercutting Campfire on price**, and it's working.

- **Ideogram** ($30K deal, lost): Rillet won on Stripe integration preference, "continuous close" feature, and shared investor (a16z). The loss notes say Rillet's integration was "preferred."
- **Succinct** ($18K deal, lost): Closed-lost reason explicitly states "10K Rillet proposal" — Rillet offered nearly half the price.
- **Novig** ($30K deal, lost): Same story — "Went with Rillet, 10K proposal we couldn't match."

**This is a pattern, not an anomaly.** Rillet is offering ~$10K proposals against Campfire's $18-30K asks. When the buyer doesn't have deep multi-entity complexity (where Campfire has a clear edge), Rillet's price wins.

**Recommended response:** Develop a competitive pricing tier or "Rillet match" offer for simpler, single-entity GL-only deals where the buyer is price-shopping. Alternatively, sharpen the value narrative around what $10K *doesn't* get you (limited integrations, no multi-entity, no AI-native close automation).

### Failure Pattern #2: Bad Qualification Is Burning Cycles

5 of the 15 losses were **obvious bad fits** that should have been disqualified earlier:

- **Willis Law Group** — law firm, not a software company (non-ICP per the attached ICP definition)
- **Bentiu Construction & Oil** — "Not a Fit"
- **Ziina** — Dubai-based, no integrated tech stack
- **Kosli** — international accounting requirements Campfire can't support
- **G5 Entertainment** — DQ'd after discovery

These deals consumed SDR and AE time without any realistic path to close. The ICP clearly states: *software companies (AI, SaaS, platforms) that do not have hardware*. Law firms, construction companies, and international non-software companies don't belong in the pipeline.

**Recommended response:** Tighten SDR qualification criteria. Add a hard ICP check before creating deal records — industry must be software/SaaS/platform, and international-only accounting requirements should be a disqualifier.

### Failure Pattern #3: "Went Cold" = Lost Executive Alignment

The "went cold" deals tell an interesting story:

- **Datatruck** had a champion (Finance Manager) but "went cold for exec align" — the champion couldn't get executive buy-in.
- **Signal Advisors** had a champion (Controller/Finance Manager, "enthusiastic about features") but still went cold.
- **Faculty** had a champion driving evaluation but no-showed the intro.

**Takeaway:** Having a finance champion is necessary but not sufficient. Deals stall when there's no **executive sponsor** above the champion. The champion feels the pain, but can't authorize the spend.

**Recommended response:** Build multi-threading into the sales process earlier. After the first positive discovery call, push for an executive alignment meeting. Ask champions directly: "Who else needs to sign off on this decision, and can we get them on the next call?"

### Failure Pattern #4: Churn Signal — Product Gaps Matter

The **Cercli renewal loss** ($16.8K) is a red flag worth examining:

> "Dissatisfaction with bugs on the platform as well as UI; ended up hiring contractor accountants to get data into platform and wasn't seeing the value"

This was a customer who churned because of **product quality issues** — bugs, poor UI, and difficulty getting data into the system. If existing customers are hiring contractors to work around platform limitations, that's a product-market signal worth escalating.

## Part 4: Recommendations for Scott's Enterprise Motion

### 1. Lead with Complexity
Your Enterprise ICP (501+ FTE, $200M-$5B ARR, Series D+) naturally has multi-entity, multi-currency, audit-readiness needs. These are Campfire's strongest differentiators and where Rillet can't compete. In every discovery call, probe for: *How many entities? What's your close timeline? Are you preparing for audit or IPO?*

### 2. Develop a "Rillet Response" Playbook
Rillet is anchoring at $10K. You need a ready-made counter:
- Quantify the cost of what $10K *doesn't* include (multi-entity, rev rec, AI close automation)
- Build an ROI calculator showing close-time savings (Campfire's continuous close vs. manual)
- Arm champions with internal justification materials they can take to their CFO

### 3. Fix Qualification Early
Work with Kyle and Ryan to enforce ICP checks before deal creation. Non-software companies, international-only accounting needs, and sub-$50M ARR companies should be routed to mid-market or disqualified. This will clean up your pipeline and focus energy on winnable deals.

### 4. Multi-Thread from Day One
The "went cold" pattern is a single-threaded problem. For every Enterprise deal, identify both the **champion** (finance operator) and the **economic buyer** (CFO/VP Finance) within the first two calls.

### 5. Double Down on Outbound + SDR
Your outbound deals (Rythm Health, Healthie, CapIntel) are winning at higher ACVs with better close rates than inbound. The SDR team is sourcing quality. Consider increasing outbound volume to your named account list, especially targeting companies with known multi-entity complexity or recent funding rounds.

### 6. Referral Pipeline Is Gold
Dealops closed at $60K in 16 days with zero competition. Systematize referral asks — after every closed-won deal, ask the champion for 2-3 introductions to peers at other companies.
