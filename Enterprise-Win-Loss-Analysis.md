# Enterprise Win/Loss Analysis — HubSpot Deal Review
**Date:** March 3, 2026 | **Analyst:** Claude (for Scott Sowers) | **Segment Focus:** Enterprise ($40K+ ACV proxy for 501+ FTE)

---

## Executive Summary

This analysis focuses exclusively on Enterprise-sized opportunities — deals with ACV of $40K+ as a proxy for the 501+ FTE segment (HubSpot lacks a direct segment property on deals). After reviewing 16 closed-won deals and 20 closed-lost deals, the Enterprise motion shows distinct dynamics from the broader book of business.

The headline: **Campfire's Enterprise win rate improves dramatically when the deal involves legacy ERP replacement, multi-entity consolidation, and a senior finance champion (Controller or CFO)**. Losses at this tier are driven less by Rillet price-undercutting (which dominates mid-market losses) and more by **organizational dysfunction** — champion turnover, board-level risk aversion, and implementation bandwidth concerns.

Average Enterprise closed-won ACV: **$53K** | Largest: **$120K (HockeyStack)** | Fastest close: **~15 days (Macabacus)**

### Enterprise Winning Pattern #1: Legacy ERP Replacement Is the #1 Close Driver

The most striking pattern at the Enterprise tier is that the majority of high-value deals involve **replacing an incumbent system**:

- **FT Partners** ($75K): Replacing **Sage Intacct** — multi-entity, multi-currency GL, data migration back to 2002
- **Censys** ($74K): Replacing **NetSuite + Maxio + bill.com** — triple platform consolidation
- **FORWARD Platform** ($50K): Replacing **NetSuite** across 3 entities
- **WeVideo** ($48K): Replacing **Sage** — including Avalara integration and contract buyout
- **Macabacus** ($40K): Replacing **Maxio** for order-to-cash and rev rec

At the Enterprise level, buyers aren't evaluating Campfire against "doing nothing" — they're in active pain with a system that's failing them. This is fundamentally different from mid-market deals where Rillet can compete on price for greenfield GL setups.

**Implication for Scott's motion:** Every Enterprise discovery call should open with: *"What are you using today, and what's breaking?"* The replacement narrative is where Campfire's value is clearest and competition is weakest.

### Enterprise Winning Pattern #2: CFO/Controller Champions Close Bigger Deals

At the Enterprise tier, champion seniority correlates directly with deal size:

| **CFO** | 1 | $75K | FT Partners (Jennifer Holly, CFO) |

| **Finance Leader/VP** | 2 | $49K avg | FORWARD Platform (Aundrea McKay), WeVideo (Brett Arnold) |

The CFO-championed deal (FT Partners) was the second-largest at $75K. Controllers appear most frequently — they're the operational buyers who feel close-process pain daily. But to win at the highest ACVs, you need executive-level sponsorship.

**Key insight:** Controllers are your door-openers. CFOs are your deal-closers. The winning motion is: land with the Controller, then get them to pull in the CFO for budget authority.

### Enterprise Winning Pattern #4: Multi-System Consolidation = Premium Pricing

The deals above $50K consistently involved consolidating multiple finance tools:

- **Censys** ($74K): NetSuite + Maxio + bill.com → Campfire (3 tools consolidated)
- **FORWARD Platform** ($50K): NetSuite → Campfire (3 entities)
- **CFI** ($50K): Core accounting + Stripe + vendor budgeting + bank feeds → Campfire
- **FT Partners** ($75K): Sage Intacct → Campfire (multi-entity, multi-currency, 20+ years of data)

When Campfire replaces **one tool**, the ACV clusters around $40-48K. When it replaces **multiple tools or consolidates entities**, the ACV jumps to $50-75K+. This is where Enterprise pricing power lives.

### Enterprise Failure Pattern #1: Organizational Dysfunction Kills More Enterprise Deals Than Price

This is the single biggest difference between Enterprise and mid-market losses. At the mid-market level, Rillet's $10K pricing is the top deal-killer. At the Enterprise level, **internal organizational issues** are the leading cause of loss:

- **Cast & Crew** ($225K — the largest lost deal in the dataset): "All DMs left org." This was an Oracle replacement opportunity where every decision-maker churned out of the company. No amount of selling can overcome wholesale champion turnover.
- **Edited Capital** ($28K): "Board not comfortable with new ERP" — Campfire was the *vendor of choice* but the board vetoed the purchase. The economic buyer couldn't secure approval from the ultimate decision-making body.
- **ProcessMaker** ($28K): "No bandwidth for implementation" — wanted Campfire but couldn't resource the project.
- **Lateral Inc** ($26K): Same story — "No bandwidth to implement."
- **Prolific** ($25K): Went cold after first demo — no champion emerged.
- **Datasnipper** ($40K): "Went Cold" — NetSuite replacement that lost momentum.

**The pattern:** Enterprise buyers often *want* Campfire but can't close internally due to champion departure, board risk-aversion, or competing priorities consuming implementation bandwidth.

**Recommended response:**
1. **Multi-thread aggressively** — never rely on a single champion. Map the org chart: champion, economic buyer, and executive sponsor
2. **Quantify the cost of delay** — give champions ammunition to fight for budget and bandwidth ("every month you delay costs $X in manual close labor")
3. **De-risk implementation** — proactively address the "bandwidth" objection with Campfire's speed-to-value story (weeks, not months)
4. **Track champion movement** — when a champion leaves, immediately reach out to their replacement and to the champion at their new company (potential new opportunity)

### Enterprise Failure Pattern #2: Rillet Competition Is Less Dominant but Still Present

At the Enterprise tier, Rillet appears in fewer losses (2 of 20, vs. dominating mid-market losses), but the pattern is consistent:

- **Ideogram** ($30K): Lost to Rillet on Stripe integration preference and shared investor (a16z). Loss notes cite Rillet's integration was "preferred."
- **Novig** ($30K): "Went with Rillet, 10K proposal we couldn't match."

Note that both of these are at the **lower end** of the Enterprise ACV range ($30K). At $50K+ deals with genuine multi-entity complexity, Rillet doesn't appear as a competitor. This reinforces the pattern: Rillet competes on price for simpler setups, but can't match Campfire's capabilities for complex Enterprise requirements.

**Takeaway:** For Scott's 501+ FTE segment, Rillet is less of a threat — but watch for it in "Enterprise-lite" deals ($25-35K) with simpler accounting needs. The best defense is qualifying for complexity early.

### Enterprise Failure Pattern #3: Product/Technical Gaps Are Real Blockers

Several Enterprise losses point to genuine product limitations:

- **Koombea** ($40K): "Localization requirements in South America" — Campfire can't support certain international accounting standards
- **Mortgage Automator** ($28K): Stripe API charge-triggering was a technical blocker
- **CoreBridge Renewal** ($25K): Churned — product quality issues driving customer away
- **Finfare** ($40K): "Wanted ridiculous buyout with low Campfire contract" — pricing/packaging dispute around NetSuite replacement terms

**Takeaway:** International accounting requirements (particularly South American/LATAM localization) and specific API integration limitations are real disqualifiers. Scott should screen for these early in discovery: *"Where are your entities? Do you need country-specific tax or compliance reporting?"*

### Enterprise Failure Pattern #4: The "Bandwidth" Objection Is an Enterprise-Specific Problem

Two deals were explicitly lost to "no bandwidth for implementation" (ProcessMaker, Lateral Inc). This doesn't appear in mid-market losses — smaller companies tend to be more agile. Enterprise buyers have longer procurement cycles, competing IT projects, and finite implementation resources.

**Recommended response:** Position Campfire's implementation timeline as a competitive advantage. Highlight that Campfire typically goes live in weeks (not the 6-12 months a NetSuite migration takes). Offer to align implementation with the buyer's fiscal calendar to reduce perceived bandwidth strain.

## Part 4: Enterprise-Specific Recommendations for Scott

### 1. Build the "ERP Replacement" Playbook for Your Segment

The data is clear: Enterprise deals close when Campfire is **replacing** Sage Intacct, NetSuite, or Maxio. For your named account list, prioritize accounts currently running these systems. Signals to look for: public complaints about their ERP on LinkedIn/G2, recent CFO hires (new finance leadership often triggers system changes), and companies approaching IPO or audit readiness (need tighter controls than their current system provides).

### 2. Solve the "Champion Turnover" Risk

The Cast & Crew loss ($225K) is a cautionary tale. At the Enterprise level, sales cycles are longer, and people leave. Your defense: multi-thread from the first meeting, document the business case in writing (so it survives personnel changes), and build relationships at multiple levels of the org. When you learn a champion is leaving, immediately reach out to (a) their replacement and (b) the champion at their new company.

### 3. Pre-Empt the "No Bandwidth" Objection

Two deals died specifically because the buyer couldn't resource implementation. Build Campfire's speed-to-value into your pitch from day one. Consider creating a one-pager: "Go live in 4 weeks, not 4 months" that addresses Enterprise buyers' implementation anxiety. Offer to align kickoff with their fiscal quarter boundary.

### 4. Qualify for Complexity — That's Your Moat

The data shows Rillet can't compete at $50K+ deals with multi-entity, multi-currency, or platform consolidation requirements. In every discovery call, probe hard for complexity: *How many entities? How many currencies? What's your current close timeline? How many systems touch your financial data?* The more complex the answer, the stronger your position.

### 5. Exploit the Outbound Advantage

Outbound Enterprise deals average $69K — nearly 50% more than inbound. Work closely with Kyle and Ryan to target your named account list with messages that reference specific pain points (e.g., "Companies replacing NetSuite are saving X days on their close"). The SDR team's outbound quality at the Enterprise tier is exceptional.

### 6. Systematize Referrals — They're Your Best Channel

Dealops ($60K in 16 days) and Speak ($40K) both came via referral with zero competition. After every Enterprise close, ask the champion: "Who do you know in your network dealing with similar finance pain?" Build this into your post-close handoff process.

### 7. Screen for International Blockers Early

Koombea was lost to South American localization requirements. For international prospects, ask in the first call: *"Do you need country-specific tax compliance, local GAAP reporting, or payroll integrations outside the US?"* If yes, flag it early rather than investing cycles in an unwinnable deal.
